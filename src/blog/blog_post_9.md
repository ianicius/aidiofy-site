# Apps That Scan Text and Turn It Into Audio: What Actually Works in 2026

**Meta Description:** Looking for apps that scan printed text and turn it into audio? We tested the main options for books, board games, and documents. Here's what works and where each one falls short.

---

You have a physical page in front of you. A book, a rulebook, a printed document. You want to hear it instead of reading it. Sounds simple. It should be simple.

So you search for an app that scans text and turns it into audio. And you find... a lot of options that each do part of the job, but none that do the whole thing without friction.

I've been through most of them. Here's what actually works, where each one breaks down, and what to use depending on what you need.

## Google Lens: fast scan, no audio

Google Lens does OCR well. Point your camera at text, and it pulls out the words almost instantly. For quick lookups and single-paragraph translations, it's unbeatable. Free, fast, already on your phone.

But it stops at text extraction. There's no read-aloud feature built in. You can copy the text and paste it into Google Translate, which has a basic TTS button, but it reads in a flat monotone with no pacing control. Fine for a sentence or two. Painful for a full page.

Best for: quick translations of short text. A card, a sign, a label.

Falls short: anything longer than a paragraph. No audio playback, no voice options.

## Microsoft Lens + Immersive Reader

Microsoft's approach combines OCR scanning with their Immersive Reader, which does have text-to-speech. The scanning is solid. Immersive Reader highlights words as they're read, which helps with following along.

The voice quality is decent for utility purposes. It reads clearly. But it reads everything the same way, because it was designed for accessibility and learning, not for narrative content. A villain's dialogue and a shopping list get the same treatment.

Best for: reading documents, study material, accessibility use cases.

Falls short: no voice variety, no role detection, treats all text the same.

## Speechify and NaturalReader

These are the big names in text-to-speech apps. Both can scan pages using OCR and read them aloud. Voice quality is good. Speed controls work well. They're built for people who want to listen to content while commuting or multitasking.

The problem? They're productivity tools. They assume you want to hear your text faster, not better. One voice reads everything. No awareness of dialogue, no character distinction, no sense of pacing for dramatic content. Feed them a chapter from a fantasy novel and they'll read it the same way they'd read a Wikipedia article.

Best for: articles, emails, PDFs, audiobook-style listening of simple text.

Falls short: narrative content, dialogue-heavy text, anything where tone matters.

## Adobe Scan + separate TTS

Adobe Scan produces clean OCR results, especially from printed pages. The app handles multi-column layouts and complex formatting better than most alternatives. You get a searchable PDF.

But that's where it ends. There's no audio built in. You'd need to export the text and feed it into a separate TTS app. The workflow is reliable but clunky: scan in one app, copy, paste into another, generate audio there.

Best for: archiving documents, getting clean text from complex layouts.

Falls short: no audio at all. Requires a second app for any read-aloud.

## So which one should you use?

Depends on what you're scanning and why.

**Quick translation of a short text**: Google Lens. Nothing beats it for speed on a single card or paragraph.

**Long documents for productivity**: Speechify or NaturalReader. Clean voices, speed controls, built for volume.

**Clean text extraction from complex pages**: Adobe Scan, then export to your TTS app of choice.

**Story-heavy content with dialogue, or text in a foreign language you want narrated**: AIdiofy. It's the only option that assigns voices to roles and handles translation and narration in one step.

**Accessibility and learning**: Microsoft Immersive Reader. The word highlighting and controlled pacing are specifically designed for this.

Most people will use more than one of these depending on the situation. Google Lens lives on my phone for quick checks. For anything with a story, characters, or dense foreign-language text, I scan it into AIdiofy because the output is actually something I want to listen to.

## The gap nobody's filled until recently

For years there's been a weird gap in this space. OCR apps got really good at extracting text. TTS apps got really good at reading text aloud. But the step in between, understanding what the text is and reading it appropriately, stayed empty.

A page from a board game scenario book is not the same as a page from a business report. A chapter with three characters speaking shouldn't sound the same as a set of instructions. Most apps ignore this distinction entirely.

That gap is closing now. The tools are getting smarter about context. And for anyone who's been scanning pages and pasting into translators and then pasting into TTS apps and still getting flat robotic output at the end of it all: yeah, it's gotten a lot better.
