# Let AI Read Your Board Game Rulebook: Translation and Narration at the Same Time

**Meta Description:** Imported board games come with long English rulebooks and scenarios. AI narration translates and reads them aloud in your language, with different voices for characters and rules.

---

If you've ever bought an imported board game, you know the feeling. Open the box, lay out the components, and then pick up the scenario book. It's all in English. And it's long.

Gloomhaven, Oathsworn, Sleeping Gods, Too Many Bones. These games sell themselves on story, but that story is written in dense English paragraphs. Reading it is tiring enough. Translating it out loud for the table is worse.

## What happens at the table

Someone reads the English and explains it to everyone. Works for the first few minutes. Then the shortcuts start. "So basically this person is saying we need to defend the village" kind of summaries. The atmosphere dies.

Another pattern: one person reads silently, translates in their head, everyone waits. Phones come out. A cooperative game turns into one person taking a reading comprehension test.

Third option: skip the story, just read the rules. The game functions mechanically, but the narrative you paid for disappears.

None of these match what you imagined when you opened the box.

## English difficulty varies wildly between games

Something like Catan? Simple English. Short card text, basic vocabulary. No issues.

But the flavor text in Too Many Bones reads like literary fiction. Even native speakers slow down for it. Legacy of Yu sits in the middle. Decent length story text with rules woven in. You're reading the narrative and suddenly mid-paragraph: "take token X and place it on space Y."

This mixing of story and rules makes translation even harder.

## What changes when AI reads it

When you scan a scenario book page with AIdiofy, it first translates into your language. So far, same as Google Translate.

The difference is what comes next. It analyzes the text structure: who's speaking, what's narration, what's a rule explanation. Then it assigns different voices to different roles.

The narrator gets a calm voice. The villain gets something low and heavy. The villager speaks normally. And when the text shifts to rules, the tone changes clearly. You can tell by listening whether you're in the story or in a game instruction.

## Why the voice shift matters

Take Legacy of Yu as an example. Within a single paragraph, the text often switches from story to rules. A tense scene where the protagonist faces a crisis, and right in the middle: "place the new token on the board."

If everything is read in the same voice and tone, you miss that switch. Or the opposite: a rule read in a storytelling voice makes you gloss over an important instruction.

When voice and tone shift automatically, everyone at the table knows "okay, this is rules now" without anyone having to confirm.

## Prep takes a few minutes

The easiest approach is to prepare before game night. Scan the scenario pages you'll play that evening and let AIdiofy process them. Takes a few minutes.

At the table, you just play the audio when you reach the next scenario section. Everyone listens to the same narration together, in their own language. Zero translation downtime. Zero "say that again" back-and-forth.

The person who always gets stuck reading finally gets to play the game like everyone else.

## Not every game needs this

Text-light games don't need it. Azul, Hanabi, anything where card text is a single line. Your phone's translation app handles that fine.

But games with scenario books running dozens of pages? Oathsworn, Sleeping Gods, Tainted Grail, Etherfields. Manually translating and reading those aloud every session is exhausting.

You bought the game to experience the story. Not to condense it into three sentences.
