# Board Game Rules Explained in Audio: How an AI Narrator Reads What You'd Rather Skip

**Meta Description:** Imported narrative board games come with walls of English text. An AI narrator reads them in your language with character voices and shifts tone between story and rules.

---

You bought an imported board game. The box is huge, the components are gorgeous, and the scenario book is eighty pages long. In English.

If you play narrative titles like Oathsworn, Sleeping Gods, Too Many Bones, or Legacy of Yu, this sounds familiar. And it's not that your English is bad. It's that some games write like they're competing for a literary award.

## What happens at the table

Someone who knows English reads aloud and translates on the fly. First five minutes go fine. Then the summaries start. "Okay so basically this guy says go north." The scene that was supposed to build tension becomes a three-sentence recap.

Another option: you read silently and translate in your head. Everyone else waits. Someone pulls out their phone. A cooperative game turns into a solo reading comprehension exercise.

Third option: skip the story text, just read the rules. The game works, but the narrative (the exact thing that justified the price) disappears.

None of these were the plan when you paid for the game.

## Not all English is the same

Some games keep it simple. Short sentences, basic vocabulary. You translate on the fly without any trouble.

Then you sit down with something like Too Many Bones, where the narrative text is written in literary English that even native speakers read slowly. Archaisms, long descriptions, sentences you need to read twice.

Legacy of Yu falls somewhere in between. Not as dense, but it has regular walls of text. Mostly story, though every few paragraphs you get instructions about new rules or components. You're reading the narrative and suddenly mid-paragraph: "take token X and place it on space Y."

That's where most people lose the thread.

## Why the voice change matters

In Legacy of Yu, within a single paragraph the text goes from a dramatic scene to a mechanical instruction. Yu is fighting for the village's survival, tension is rising, and suddenly: "place the new token on the board."

If everything is read with the same voice and tone, you miss that switch. Or the reverse: a dry instruction read with dramatic voice makes you gloss over something important.

When voice and tone shift automatically at the right moment, the whole table understands what's what. Nobody needs to ask "was that part of the story or do I need to do something?"
