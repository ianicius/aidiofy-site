# Always the One Translating Rules? How to Stop Being the Table's Unpaid Narrator

**Meta Description:** If you're always the one reading and translating board game text at the table, you know the toll it takes. An AI narrator can take over so you can actually play.

---

Every group has that person. The one who speaks the best English (or just didn't say no fast enough). The one who gets handed the scenario book at every imported game and hears: "so, read it out."

If that's you, then you know what it actually looks like.

## Translator, reader, narrator, and rules judge rolled into one

On paper it sounds easy: read the English text, translate it, pass it along. In practice you're doing five things at once.

Reading. Translating in your head. Trying to convey the mood, because this is supposed to be a story scene, not assembly instructions. Simultaneously filtering what's narrative and what's a rule. And also answering questions like "wait, am I supposed to do something now or is this still the story part?"

After an hour you're drained. Not because the game is boring. Because instead of playing, you've been working.

## The problem nobody talks about out loud

Nobody complains, because from the outside it looks harmless. "Hey, read what it says here." What's the big deal, right?

The big deal is that live translation eats all your attention. You're not tracking strategy. You're not planning moves. You're not reacting to the story the way the rest of the table does, because for you it's not a narrative. It's raw material to process.

In cooperative games it's manageable. But in games where you have your own goals and decisions? You lose your edge, because half your brain is translating text instead of thinking about the game.

And there's something worse: after a few sessions like this, you start cutting corners. Skip descriptions. Summarize dialogue. "Basically this guy says we should go north." Because you don't have the energy for nuance after two hours of nonstop translation.

The story that designers spent months crafting reaches the rest of the table in an abridged version.

## "But I like translating"

Maybe you do. For the first scenario. Maybe the second.

But by the fifth evening in a row, staring at another four pages of Legacy of Yu or Sleeping Gods, that feeling turns into obligation. You're not playing a board game anymore. You're running a one-person translation office at the table.

And the rest of the group doesn't even realize how much effort goes into it. Because from their side it looks like this: you sit down, read, say it in their language. Simple.

Not someone from the group. A narrator. One that reads the text in your language, with different voices for different roles, at a good pace. One that recognizes when the narrator speaks, when a character speaks, and when a rules section starts.

You just listen. Same as everyone else at the table.

## You don't have to prep this at the table

The most convenient approach is to handle it before the session. Scan the scenario texts you'll play that evening. AIdiofy processes them in a few minutes. At the table you just play the audio when you reach the next section.

No reading. No live translation. No "wait, say that last sentence again."

You can finally focus on the game instead of the text.

## The rest of the table benefits too

When you translate live, it's your version of the text. With your shortcuts, your pacing, your interpretation. Sometimes you skip something, sometimes you rearrange, sometimes you lose the thread.

When the text is read from start to finish, with proper structure and voice casting, everyone at the table gets the same complete version. Nobody has to ask "what did he say?" or "was that important or can I ignore it?"

Listening together also does something else: it sets the mood. When a menacing antagonist speaks in a threatening voice and the narrator calmly describes the scenery, it hits the whole group at once. You can't achieve that translating from a book on your knee.
