# Walls of Text in Board Games: How an AI Narrator Reads What You'd Rather Skip

**Meta Description:** Narrative board games like Legacy of Yu and Too Many Bones have walls of English text mixing story with rules. An AI narrator reads them aloud with role-based voices, so you don't have to.

---

You open the box. Punch out the tokens, lay down the board. Then you pick up the scenario book and hit four pages of dense text. Dialogue, location descriptions, and somewhere buried in the middle, rules for a new component that just entered the game.

Now what?

If you play narrative imports (Oathsworn, Sleeping Gods, Too Many Bones, Legacy of Yu), you know exactly what I mean. And it's not that your English is bad. It's that some games write like they're targeting English lit graduates.

## Not all English is created equal

Some games keep it simple. Short sentences, clear instructions, standard vocabulary. You translate on the fly and it's fine.

Then you sit down with a game where the English suddenly reads like Victorian prose. Too Many Bones is a prime example. The story text uses a style that even native speakers slow down for. Archaisms, long descriptions, sentences you need to read twice.

Legacy of Yu sits somewhere in between. Not as dense as TMB, but there are regular walls of text to get through. Mostly story, but every few paragraphs the game throws in new rules or component instructions. You're reading the narrative, getting into it, and suddenly mid-paragraph: "now take token X and place it on space Y."

That's where most players lose the thread.

## What actually happens at the table

Option A: whoever speaks the best English reads aloud and translates on the fly. Works for the first five minutes. Then comes the stumbling, the shortcuts, the "so basically what this means is..." and half the atmosphere goes out the window. A scene that should build tension turns into a three-sentence summary.

Option B: you read silently and translate in your head. Everyone else waits. Someone checks their phone. Someone else sorts tokens. A group game becomes a solo reading comprehension exercise.

Option C: skip the story text, just read the rules. The game works mechanically, but the narrative (the exact thing you paid for) disappears.

None of these were the plan when you spent $80 backing the Kickstarter.

## Why the voice change at rules matters

You're listening to an emotional scene. Yu is fighting for the survival of the village. Tension is building. Then, same paragraph, there's information about a new token and a rule entering play.

If the narrator reads everything in one voice at one tone, you'll miss that transition. Or the opposite happens: a dry mechanical instruction kills the mood because it sounds like part of the novel.

When the narrator detects a shift from story to rules, the tone changes automatically. Story sections sound like a tale being told. Rules sections sound like clear, direct instructions. You don't have to figure out yourself where the story ends and the game mechanics begin.

In Legacy of Yu these transitions happen constantly, often mid-paragraph. That's exactly why this feature makes such a big difference there.

## Walls of text aren't the problem. They're the content you paid for

Narrative games are expensive. Often $60-100 with overseas shipping. You buy them precisely because they have rich storylines, atmospheric descriptions, characters with personality. Those "walls of text" aren't a flaw. They're the entire point.

The problem only starts when a language barrier turns that content into an obstacle instead of a pleasure.

Google Lens and DeepL work fine for a single card or a short paragraph. But when you're facing four pages of scenario with dialogue, descriptions, and embedded rules, you need something that doesn't just translate but reads it properly. With voice casting. At a good pace. With a clear tone shift when the text moves from story to mechanics.
